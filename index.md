# Conclusion

The data clearly shows that students who have a lower understanding find the exercises to be less effective, require more office hour visits, and view algorithms as less objective. Based on the data, it is logical to conclude that if students had a better grasp on the exercises, their understanding would increase. Therefore, it is imperative that exercises be made more effective, which could be accomplished by making them more focused on the actual coding rather than the nitpicking of the autograder. The nitpicking of the autograder can be assumed to be an issue for those who have a lower understanding based on the relationship between understanding and views on algorithm objectiveness. Objectivity is often associated with fairness, and so those who have a lower understanding (and therefore believe the exercises to be less effective) would think the algorithm that is autograder is not being fair. If the instructions were clearer on what the autograder expected, then this issue would be remedied, and it is likely that there would be an increase in viewing algorithms as objective as a result of implementing this idea. As expected, there was a correlation between those who went to office hours more frequently and those who had a lower understanding and found the exercises to be less effective. A logical reason for students who don’t view the exercises as effective to go to office hours would be if they thought the expectations for the autograder were unclear, and were therefore seeking help figuring out how to cater to what the autograder wanted. If the idea were to be incorporated, then the need for students to attend office hours for the trivial reason of the autograder’s expectations would be erased, and that time could be used to help students who have technical issues or issues with the core aspects of the exercises.
There are some possible downsides to the implementation of this idea, yet they are all avoidable if done right. If some students view the autograder’s expectations as something akin to a puzzle to piece together, then by making the exercises more clear, their enjoyment could be lessened. However, the likelihood of a majority of the students feeling this way is incredibly low, but maybe it would be a detriment to the enjoyability of the course for some students. Furthermore, if handled improperly, implementing this idea could trivialize the exercises by just straight up giving the students the solutions to what the autograder wants, but if enough thought is put into it, this pitfall can easily be avoided. There are also other ways for the purpose behind this idea to be achieved. A way a similar goal could be reached would be by having the autograder’s feedback be more clear. In other words, have the feedback be in more straightforward English, rather than code that the students have to decipher.
